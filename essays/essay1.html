Essay 1

UI & UX ANALYSIS

INTRODUCTION

As South Africa’s largest online retailer, Takealot offers a wide variety of products to consumers. (Takealot, 2019) Takealot plays a major role in the e-commerce market, and its website needs to balance its commercial goals with user experience (UX) and user interface (UI) design. Drawing from readings and lecture materials, this essay will be an analysis of Takealot’s UI and UX, evaluating both its functional effectiveness and its ethical implications. This essay argues that while Takealot’s website effectively enhances usability and customer engagement, its interaction design raises ethical concerns about data privacy, manipulative dark patterns, and potential exclusionary design.

CONTEXT

Takealot’s website and mobile app serve as the core platform for its business operations, as the company relies exclusively on its digital storefront for sales and customer engagement. The platform offers an extensive product catalogue with a wide range of categories, including groceries, fashion, and many others. Once an order is placed, customers can choose between home delivery or collection from a nearby Takealot pickup point. (Takealot, 2019) Takealot was founded in 2011, and its platform has grown exponentially since with over 10 million monthly website visits. (Statista, n.d.) This growth has been created by the platform’s strategic emphasis on rapid delivery services, competitive pricing, and the website's user experience and user interface design.

UI DESIGN

Takealot’s website utilizes UI design principles to optimize usability and drive sales. UI refers to the visual and interactive elements of a website that enable the user to interact with it. (Collins, 2023) The interface features a clean, minimalistic layout with intuitive navigation. This section will analyse Takealot’s navigation structure, colour palette, web content accessibility guidelines and visual hierarchy.

NAVIGATION DESIGN & PROMOTIONAL ELEMENT

The website features a top navigation bar with a search bar, category menu, account options, cart and daily deals section. This navigation bar is designed to provide quick and easy access to the most important areas of the website. The search bar stands out through its size and placement, allowing users to quickly locate specific products they are interested in. It displays autocomplete suggestions of product names, trending searches, or spelling corrections to cut down search time and to promote tending items. The category menu condenses multiple categories into a single button, when the button is clicked on it creates a drop-down menu. The drop-down menu avoids cluttering the interface and overwhelming users with information. The cart icon uses a universally recognized symbol of a cart, allowing users to intuitively understand its purpose without needing to learn new visual languages. According to Raffee, familiar icons improve user experience by providing instant recognition. (Raffee, 2025, pg. 9) The cart icon leads the user to a page that displays the products a user has selected for purchase, offering a visual summary of their shopping activity. The daily deals section is strategically placed in the navigation bar to continuously drive sales. Because the navigation appears on every page, it ensures that users are constantly reminded of current promotions. The website heavily promotes sales, as seen in Figure 1 where they are promoting an Easter sale. The Easter sale is a large visually striking image which takes up the middle of the page, capturing the user’s attention. The prominent use of purple against the plain white background increases the visual impact, making the promotion an obvious focal point. The words “Save” and “55%” are displayed in large, bold white text. The use of the word “save” acts as a psychological trigger, creating a sense of urgency and excitement. Research shows how “discounts tap into our brain’s reward system, making us feel pleasure and excitement when we see a good deal.” (Wise, 2022) This constant remind of promotions is used as a tactic for users to purchase products, but this topic will be expanded on more in later parts of this essay.

COLOUR PALETTE & BRANDING

In the previous paragraph, the importance of colour in web design was discussed. As stated by Raffee in her lecture slides “colour plays a vital role in web design, influencing branding, user experience, and visual hierarchy.” (Raffee, 2025, pg.13) Takealot’s website uses a minimalist and consistent colour palette, primarily featuring blue (#0077C2), white (#FFFFFF), and shades of grey. Blue, the dominant colour in the branding and UI, appears in the navigation bar, clickable links, and buttons. It conveys trust and loyalty, crucial for an e-commerce platform that requires sensitive customer information. White is the main background, creating a clean, spacious layout that makes text and product images stand out clearly. Shades of grey are used for secondary text and borders, maintaining a minimalist aesthetic without drawing attention away from key content. Product prices are displayed in black, reinforcing their importance in the visual hierarchy. Red and green act as accent colours: red highlights urgency in sales banners and error messages, while green is typically used to indicate positive actions like successful transactions. (Wise, 2022) These choices ensure a cohesive design that supports branding, enhances user trust and improves navigation.

THE WEB CONTENT ACCESSIBILITY GUIDELINES

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are the international standards designed to make web content more accessible to people with disabilities. (World Wide Web Consortium, 2024) These guidelines focus on the content being perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust. Perceivable is the first principle that ensures users can perceive and access web content. WCAG 2.1 guidelines require non-text content to have text alternatives (W3C, 2018). Takealot’s website provides alternative text for most of its products, as seen in Figure 2, which provides a detailed alternative text for an image of an SSD card. However, some of their promotional banners lack descriptive alternative texts. Alt text is essential for users relying on screen readers and when there is a lack of alternative texts, it can reduce accessibility for visually impaired users. The operable principle ensures that users can interact with and navigate a website effectively, even when just using keyboards, assistive technology, or voice commands. (W3C, 2018) The navigation on Takealot is keyboard-friendly, but on some dropdown menus, it is difficult to navigate without a menu. The third principle is understandable which ensures that web content is clear for users with cognitive, learning, and language disabilities. This emphasizes the importance of readable and predictable content. (W3C, 2018) The consistent layout of the website allows users to get used to one layout for the website and not have to constantly learn new layouts. On certain products, there is a product description that avoids complex jargon allowing for many users to understand. However, with some products like the one in Figure 3, there is a lack of description, leaving users confused and needing to do further research. The last principle is robust, which ensures that web content is compatible with assistive technologies such as voice control, braille displays, and screen readers. (W3C, 2018) Takealot has constant updates to ensure that it supports a wide range of assistive technologies.

UX DESIGN

UX (User Experience) design refers to the process used to create meaningful and relevant experiences for users. It focuses on understanding user needs, behaviours, and emotions to design websites that are functional and accessible. (Interaction Design Foundation, 2016) This part of the essay will be analysing the user experience of the Takealot website through the different laws and effects of UX design.

LAWS

This section will explore key psychological principles, such as the Von Restorff Effect, Hick’s Law, and the Zeigarnik Effect, and how they are implemented within the UX design of the Takealot website to enhance user interaction. The Von Restorff Effect predicts that an object is most likely going to be remembered when it differs from objects that are similar. (Raffee, 2025, pg.3) In Figure 4, the users’ eyes are led straight to the “takealot more deals” category, as it differs from the categories above it. It has a different font, and the red border creates this category to be a focal point. As seen in Figure 1, there are many categories for the user to go through and it can take quite a bit of time as there are so many choices. This leads us to Hick’s law, where the amount of time to make a decision is dependent on the number of choices provided to the user. (Raffee, 2025, pg. 7) While searching through categories and adding products to the cart, users are introduced to cart reminders. Cart reminders are sent through an email or push notification, to remind users of items left in their cart and encourage them to come back and complete their purchases. This is known as the Zeigarnik effect which looks at how users “remember uncompleted takes better than completed tasks.” (Raffee, 2025, pg.9) All these laws are used throughout the website to drive engagement and enhance user interaction.

ETHICS OF INTERACTION DESIGN

Ethics in interaction design refers to the moral principles that guide designers when creating a digital experience. (Mahdy Farahany, 2025) This discussion will look at how the interaction design of Takealot’s website raises concerns about design justice and dark patterns.

DESIGN JUSTIC PERSPECTIVE

Through Sasha Costanza-Chock’s reading, “Design Justice,” we will be evaluating the concept of design justice within the Takealot website. “Design justice rethinks design processes, centers people who are normally marginalized by design, and uses collaborative, creative practices to address the deepest challenges our communities face.” (Sasha Constanza-Chock, 2020, pg.6). This quote discusses a way of designing which prioritizes inclusion and equity. As discussed in previous parts of the essay, Takealot website has created an easy-to-navigate shopping experience for many users, through its UX and UI design. However, there is a lack of evidence that shows that Takealot’s design is centered around marginalized users. Takealot needs to include marginalized users in their design process, allowing users to help redesign features. As discussed previously in this essay, Takealot is compliant with the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines, but it is still lacking in certain areas. An example is the complex descriptions of products, which contain jargon that many users do not understand. Costanza-Chock’s reading focuses heavily on prioritizing the “design’s impact on the community over the intentions of the designer.” (Sasha Constanza-Chock, 2020, pg.6) We can see the intentions of the designers to try to make a website that is accessible for more users, but the website is so focused on profits than helping marginalized communities. Takealot has a subscription program called Takealot One, where users pay for a monthly or yearly subscription. This gives the benefits of free delivery services, priority access, and exclusive discounts. This subscription plan excludes users who cannot afford it. It gives better discounts and free delivery to users who can afford it.

DARK PATTERNS PERSPECTIVE

Through the website “Dark Patterns at Scale” we will evaluate how Takealot uses dark patterns and deceptive design in its website. Before diving into this topic we need to understand what dark patterns are, the authors of this website describe them as “user interface design choices that benefit an online service by coercing, steering or deceiving users.” (Mathur et al., 2019) As discussed in the previous topic about design justices, Takealot has a subscription plan that is easy to opt into but is harder to unsubscribe from. This is a characteristic of a hard-to-cancel pattern, where canceling a Takealot One subscription involves several steps that can be hard to understand, making it a challenging process for some users. Takealot contains a hidden cost dark pattern where some fees or delivery costs are only revealed later in the checkout process. This tactic is used so that users can spend the extra money, as they are excited about the products they are about to purchase. Takealot also contains a Fake scarcity dark pattern, which is when “the user is pressured into completing an action because they are presented with a fake indication of limited supply or popularity.” (Mathur et al., 2019) This tactic is seen throughout the Takealot’s website where they have text stating “Only 3 left!” or “Limited time offer!”

CONCLUSION

Takealot’s website demonstrates a strong use of UI and UX design principles that contribute to its usability and commercial success. Its strategic use of colour and intuitive design all contribute to an engaging shopping experience. However, while the platform showcases effective design, it brings up some ethical issues. Issues such as limited accessibility for marginalized users, the use of manipulative dark patterns, and the prioritization of profit over community-centered design. This analysis highlights that while Takealot succeeds in creating an efficient digital marketplace, it needs to address ethical issues to create a more inclusive user experience.
