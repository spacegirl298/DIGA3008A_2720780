Essay 2

The Digital Silencing of Palestinian Voices: How geopolitics & Digital Coloniality Play a Role in Internet Censorship

The ushering in of the digital age and the Internet has fundamentally changed the state of global communication and interaction. This rapid growth in interconnectedness is not neutral, because of the largely centralized and controlled by large corporations it is profoundly influenced by geopolitical dynamics and often times perpetuates forms of “digital coloniality”, which is when dominant powers control digital spheres in order to reinforce existing inequalities and narratives. The widespread censorship of current affairs and news content related to the Palestinian situation (genocide) on platforms like Tik Tok is a compelling case study. It illustrates how online platforms can become tools in international conflicts, aiding to silence marginalized voices and controlling global perceptions. This essay explores how the internet, geopolitics and digital coloniality intersect through Tik Toks content moderation concerning the Palestinian conflict (genocide).

As the conflict in Gaza escalated and reached worldwide audiences in 2024, Palestinian voices across social media platforms experienced a surge in digital repression (El Houri, 2025). A report from Sada Social, a palestinian digital rights organisation, brings light to a troubling truth, documenting over 25, 000 violations against Palestinian digital content across major social platforms including Tik Tok in 2024 (El Houri, 2025). Tik Tok accounted for 27 percent of these instances (El Houri, 2025, p.3). These violations came in the form of “content takedowns, shadow banning, account suspensions, and other forms of algorithmic suppression” (El houri, 2025, p.2). Users reported the restrictions of posts sharing footage from Gaza, mourning martyrs, or expressing political solidarity, with some even being “locked out of their accounts entirely” (El Houri, 2025, p.2). The censorship went beyond images of the massacres to include content related to the assassination of Ismail Haniyeh, a palestinian politician, and protests with anti-occupation slogans such as “Death to Israel” and “Death to America”, thereby hindering the work of media organisations and violating core principles of press freedom (El Houri, 2025, p.5)

The geopolitical underpinnings of TikTok’s censorship are evident in the discourse that surrounded the potential ban in the United States in 2024 and its subsequent ban in 2025. While the ban was publicly framed as a national security concern due to its Chinese ownership, congressional insiders and Israeli officials revealed a link to concerns about Israel’s public image (MEE staff, 2025). US Senator Mark Warner and former Congressman Mike Gallagher, who introduced the bill against TikTok disclosed that the legislation ‘gained legs again’ after October 7th, when ‘people started to see a bunch of anti semitic content the platform’ (MEE staff, 2025, p.284). Ken Klippenstein, an independent journalist, reported that Israeli officials and lobbyists explicitly informed Washington insiders about Tik Tok’s significant influence on young Americans' views of Israel (MEE staff, 2025, p. 285). This perception directly played in role in influencing US lawmakers, with figures such as Mitt Romney expressing ‘overwhelming support for us to shut down potentially Tik Tok’ due to the ‘disproportionately high number of mentions of Palestinians on the platform relative to other social media sites’ (MEE staff, 2025, p.287). Nikki Haley even went as far as to suggest that users merely engaging with content on Tik Tok could make them antisemitic (MEE staff, 2025, p. 287). This demonstrates how geopolitical interests can directly influence decisions regarding online content moderation and platform availability, transforming digital spaces into crucial battlegrounds for narrative control.

The silencing of Palestinian voices on platforms such as TikTok and other social media, serves as a stark example of digital coloniality, where dominant powers impose control over the flow of digital data (Fatafta, 2023; Rajagopal,2023). This control is a reflection of existing offline power imbalances (El Houri, 2025; Robinson et al., 2015). Sada Social’s report highlights how content moderation "disproportionately disfavoured and silenced Arabic-language political speech, which are influenced by post-9/11 ‘war on terror’ policies (Fatafta, 2023, p.21). There is a significant double standard- while Palestinian content is often censored, the report noted that there was an ‘unchecked spread of hate speech and incitement against Palestinians’, often by Israeli accounts, including ‘explicit calls for genocide’ (El Houri, 2025, p6). There is a stark contrast between the response to the Palestinian situation (genocide) vs. the war on Ukraine, where rules where ‘bent’ and citizens where allowed to express ‘resistance and fury’ against invaders, including calls for violence. These privileges were not extended to Palestinians (Fatafta, 2023, p. 23-24). The reliance on automated content moderation tools, which have been poorly trained in Arabic and incorrectly deleted nonviolent Arabic content 77% of the time, further demonstrates systemic bias and unfairness (Fatafta, 2023, p26). Issues like Al-Aqsa Mosque being labelled a terrorist organisation or Palestinian terrorists’ underscore these systemic failures (Fatafta, 2023, p27). The digital repression of Palestinians is not confined to specific geographies, indicating a systemic global effort (El houri, 2025, p.9). The broader concept of digital inequality suggests that groups that are already subject to inequalities offline often experience less internet usage and face barriers when it comes to effective digital engagement, which amplifies their disadvantage in conflict contexts by limiting their ability to document human rights abuses and share their stories( Robinson et al., 2015, p. 140). The theoretical understanding of the internet’s impact on geographic location as discussed in the sources, further illuminates the inherent complexities of digital spaces (Greenstein et al., 2018). The internet reduces three key interrelated economic frictions; communication costs, transportation costs, and search costs (Greenstein et al., 2018, p. 38). This initially gives the impression that there has been a leveling of the playing field (Greenstein et al. 2018, p. 43). However it is becoming increasingly evident that online platforms are not neutral (El Houri, 2025, p. 10). Instead, in contexts of conflict, “algorithms, reporting mechanisms, and moderation practices become part of the conflict itself” (El Houri, 2025, p.10). This demonstrates that while the internet theoretically fosters greater connectivity and information sharing, in practice, it can be leveraged to reinforce existing power imbalances and inequalities. The selective enforcement of content policies as seen with Tik Tok and other platforms when it comes to the Palestinian case provides evidence that the “death of distance” model is “too simplistic” (Greenstein et al., 2018, p. 81). Offline differences and geopolitical interests significantly mediate how digital technology shapes information access and expression (Greenstein et al., 2018, p. 81).

In conclusion, the case of TikToks censorship of Palestinian content strikingly demonstrates the intersections between the internet, geopolitics, and digital coloniality. Online platforms are far from being neutral carriers of information, and are often deeply embedded in global power dynamics, their content moderation policies and algorithmic biases reflecting and reinforcing geopolitical interests. The digital erasure of Palestinian narratives, including documentation of alleged war crimes illustrates a tangible form of digital coloniality, where the dominant narrative is systemically enforced. The intentional silencing is often influenced and fueled by powerful corporate interests, which ultimately limits global understanding and exacerbates existing inequalities by denying marginalized communities their right to digital expression and documentation, As the digital landscape continues evolve, understanding these complex interplays is crucial for advocating for genuine rights and fostering a more equitable and inclusive global information environment.
