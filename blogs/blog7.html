Disconnected & Disadvantaged: Justice in a Digital World

“The Need for Global Internet Connectivity” – Claire Daniolou

This blog will be a critical engagement of Claire Daniolou’s reading. In her reading she argues that internet access is a necessity, she frames it as a tool to reduce marginalization, foster economic, educational and cultural growth.

The Digital Divide

The internet is not merely just about entertainment. It is a gateway to education, healthcare, and economic opportunity. Daniolou points out that rural areas often face a “rural penalty” (Daniolou, 2020, pg.2), which is an isolation that stifles cultural and economical growth. Without access to the internet, these communities miss out on everything that the digital world provides. Danilou warns that “technological illiteracy can be even more harmful than no internet at all.” (Daniolou, 2020, pg.3) This means that handing out laptops and smartphones is not enough. People need to be educated on how to use it.

Connectivity as a Catalyst

Initiatives like the EU’s digital strategies and GoGlobal’s e-commerce programs are showing just how transformative internet access can be. As Daniolou puts it, broadband enables “fast and efficient communication with anyone around the globe” (Danioloi, 2020, pg. 2), breaking down barriers that once kept rural entrepreneurs, students, and communities isolated.

Dark Side of the Internet

Daniolou notes that “online information can often be misleading” (Danioloi, 2020, pg. 2), stressing the need for critical thinking. The internet contains misinformation, cultural homogenization, and even online radicalization are real dangers.

Moving Forward

A solution for these issues is that we need universal access and we need digital literacy programs to ensure people can navigate the internet safely. We also need address ethical issues in the internet world, ensuring that tech serves humanity and not the other way around.

“Internet Access is a Fundamental Right. Especially, Amidst a Pandemic” – Spyros Kasapis

This blog will engage with Kasapis’s reading. The COVID-19 pandemic did not just disrupt our lives, it exposed inequalities in the digital realm. Spyros argues that the internet turned from a convenience into a necessity.

Digital Divide

Everybody was locked in their houses, and their only access to communication with the outside world was through the internet. University, schools and workplaces went online, so that people did not lose this time. Kasapis highlights in her reading how lockdown forced education, work, and commerce online, leaving those without internet access to be left out and lacking tools. The pandemic didn’t create this divide, it just made it impossible to ignore.

Is Internet Access a Human Right?

Some people argue the internet is a tool to exercise, while others declare it is a fundamental right itself. I personally feel like internet access is a human right, because it is a gateway to modern life. People need it for education, jobs, healthcare, and even social connections, which is essential for opportunities. The pandemic proved this, when everything moved online, those without access were left behind, deepening inequality.

Final Thoughts

The pandemic proved internet access is not a luxury, it is a necessity for the digital age. Whether we classify it as a right or a utility, the stakes are the same, exclusion fuels inequality.
