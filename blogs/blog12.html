Embracing Ethical Responsibility in Art and Code

Introduction

In this blog I will be discussing my thoughts on approaching justice and ethics in artistic and programming practices. This blog will be grounded in my personal reflections and insights drawn from the reading “Values at Play: Design Tradeoffs in Socially-Oriented Game Design.” In my journey of becoming an artist and programmer, I have come to realise that every creative decision I make is inherently political. With every piece of work that I create, I am encoding values into my work, whether it is intentional or not. “Values are everywhere, embedded in every aspect of our culture and lurking in the very nature of our media and our technologies” (Flanagan, Howe and Nissenbaum, 2005, pg. 758). The idea that all of my work carries embedded values is quite mind-boggling. But with this awareness, I can begin to approach my creations more deliberately, while also allowing myself the grace to make mistakes, knowing that I’m still learning and growing in this practice.

Values are Always Present

Many people assume that technology and art are neutral, but this isn’t true. Flanagan shows in his reading that even small design choices, like how a game character looks or how a program works, can have ethical effects. For example, their RAPUNSEL project was designed to teach girls programming in a way that encouraged collaboration and creativity, challenging the stereotype that coding is only for boys (Flanagan, Howe and Nissenbaum, 2005, pg.752). This idea makes me think more carefully about my own projects. The Values in Design framework (Flanagan, Howe and Nissenbaum, 2005, pg.753) taught me that ethics should be an afterthought, it should be part of the process from the beginning.

Including Other in the Process

One important lesion from the reading is the value of working with the people will use what I create. The RAPUNSEL team talked to teachers, students and parents to make sure their game was fair and empowering (Flanagan, Howe and Nissenbaum, 2005, pg.756). During the process of creating my website I tried to do the same, by asking for feedback from friends, lecturers and family members. This helps give me multiple perspectives of what people would want to see in a website.

Conclusion

In reflecting on justice and ethics in my artistic and programming practices, I’ve come to understand that every creative choice I make carries embedded values, whether I intend it to or not. The reading Values at Play has reinforced the idea that technology and art are never neutral; even the smallest design decisions can have ethical implications. By recognizing this, I can approach my work with greater intentionality, ensuring that my projects align with principles of fairness, inclusivity, and social responsibility.

References

Flanagan, M., Howe, D. C., & Nissenbaum, H. (2005). Values at Play: Design Tradeoffs in Socially-Oriented Game Design. CHI 2005 Proceedings, 751–760.
