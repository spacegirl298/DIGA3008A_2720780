Close Readig of Spyros Kasapis's Reading

Introduction

In Spyros Kasapis article, “Internet access is a Fundamental Right,” he argues that internet access is not a merely a modern convenience but an essential human right, especially when bringing in the topic of the Covid-19 pandemic. Global lockdowns forced people to migrate their daily activities, and this digital divide highlighted structural inequality. This blog will be a close reading of Kasapis’s article, critically examining his aims, methodology, key arguments and conclusion, while situating his argument within broader discussions about digital inclusion and the responsibilities of governments and corporations in ensuring access to technology.

SUMMARY

Kasapis’s text argues that access to the internet should be considered a basic human right, he discusses how the COVID-19 pandemic highlights these inequalities. He compares internet access to other recognised rights (such as education, healthcare and employment), which increasingly depend on digital connectivity. He emphasizes that the pandemic has intensified people’s dependence on technology, which excludes people from education and access to critical information, due to their lack of access to technology or the internet. He critiques the capitalistic structures that create and deepen this digital divide and call for an equitable digital society governed by fair rules.

Aims

The primary aim of the article is to argue that access to the internet should be recognised as a basic human right in this digital age. He states in his reading that “half the planet does not have equal access to education, health (through digital information) and the labor market.” (Kasapis, 2020, pg. 2) This raises concerns about how the lack of internet access reinforces existing inequalities and limits people to fully participate in society. He aims to examine these issues specifically in the context of times of crisis, like the COVID-19 pandemic. He seeks to raise awareness about the digital divide and push for political and social change.

Methodology

Kasapis took a philosophical and normative approach to write this article, where he combines critiquing socio-politics and moral reasoning.

  • His argument is grounded in concepts of fairness, justice and human rights. “Lack of food, education, health care and fair working conditions are obstacles to achieving happiness. Therefore, all of the above are – like happiness- human rights.” (Kasapis, 2020, pg. 2) This quote is an example of Kasapis using ethical argumentation.
  • Kasapis uses rhetorical questions in his reading, to encourage internal and governmental reflection.
  • Kasapis uses context from the real world, such as the COVID-19 pandemic. This allows the reader to relate to the topic, as we all experienced the struggles of the pandemic, but it also gives a perspective to other people’s lives during the pandemic. This opens readers eyes to their privilege as most didn’t experience these issues. His articles demonstrate how global crises reveals the need for internet access.
  • He critiques the inequality in access and control. He discusses how the rich keep getting wealthier, while half of the world population don’t have access to this wealth and power. This is a critique of power and capitalism.

Structure

Introduction:

  • He opens this article by linking happiness to human rights and introduces the topic of internet access.

Body:

  • He introduces the topic of the COVID-19 pandemic, to discuss how internet access is tied to social participation and survival.
  • He shifts to critique structural inequality, where our capitalistic society has been set up to enrich some and exclude most from the digital world.

Conlusion:

  • The article does not end with a usual conclusion but ends with rhetorical question which push readers to reflect on their moral and political responsibility.

Key Argument and Results

  • His central argument is that internet access is not a privilege it is a fundamental human right. He connects it with other essential rights such as education, healthcare, and employment.
  • He critiques how the capitalist systems that are emplaced concentrate wealth and digital access to a select few, where half of the world remains disconnected.
  • He calls for state intervention, where the government needs to take moral responsibility. He states that digital access is not just a technological issue but a matter of justice.
  • He exposes the digital divide that occurred during the COVID-19 pandemic, where the lack of internet and digital access heightens inequality. He argues that the pandemic became opened people’s eyes to how essential digital access is and how important it is for participation in society.
  • Kasapis has many key arguments, but he does not necessarily offer results. He does not give statistics to his research, but he leaves the reader with a message to reflect on. The message is that the internet is a basic need, and everyone should have access to it.

Critical Reflection

Kasapis presents a string argument that internet access should be a considered a human right:

  • He effectively argues that internet access is essential for participating in our modern society. He uses the COVID-19 pandemic as an example to further argue his point and to show how the lack of internet and digital access deepens inequality.
  • He raises important ethical questions and challenges readers to think about justice, fairness and digital inclusion.
  • However, there is no clear solution offered. He calls for change but doesn’t explain how the government or people could realistically make a change.
  • The article could have made a stronger argument by adding data from different countries and classes. He is very general with his argument and doesn’t explore how to work around or with the systems that are already emplaced.

Relation to Additional Research

United Nations: Internet is Human Right

  • The UN declared that internet access is tied to human rights. (Francisco, 2012)

The deepening divide – Jan A.G.M. van Dijk

  • In Dijk’s article he argues that those with lower income or education levels use the internet less effectively, which adds to Kasapis’s point that access to the internet alone is not enough, there needs to be equal opportunities in the digital space.

  • Researching into the need for internet and digital access before and after the access. Studying the difference between the need for this access.

Studies on Free Internet Programs

  • Researching into countries that have tried to expand access.

Private companies and the role of the government

  • Exploring the responsibilities of private companies and the role of the government.
