Designing a Moral Web: Toward an Ethical Internet

My idea of an ethical internet is a space that fosters inclusivity, responsibility, genuine connection and does not harm or exclude users. This blog will be an exploration of my ideas of an ethical internet and will be drawing from Shani Orgad’s reading, The internet as a moral space: the legacy of Roger Silverstone, to further explore the ideas of an ethical internet.

One concept that Shani Orgad mentions in his reading, is Roger Silverstone’s idea of “proper distance” (Orgad, 2007, pg. 34). This idea encourages finding a right balance between closeness and detachment in online interactions to foster moral responsibility. Creating it to be space where users are neither so distance that they ignore other’s humanity or so close that they exploit or overwhelm other users. Currently, many social media platforms prioritize engagement at all costs, which amplifies polarization and outrage. Social media platforms could design algorithms that prioritize meaningful dialogue over sensationalism. This would help people connect better and reduce arguments and division. Platforms could introduce controlled exposure to diverse perspective, to not overwhelm users, but to humanize. By redesigning algorithms, we can reduce polarization and encourage empathy.

The internet is a space where many can feel like they are in an inhospitable environment. As Silverstone notes, the “click of a mouse” can exclude voices that don’t conform (Orgad, 2007, pg. 38). The way that the internet has been designed has allowed for people to have freedom of speech, but within this freedom of speech users have been ostracized for speaking up. This creates an environment where many users, especially those from minority groups, feel unwelcome, unsafe, or silenced. My vision of an ethical internet actively uplifts marginalized voices instead of silencing them. This could be done through changing algorithms, creating safe spaces and holding people accountable. Algorithms could be altered to boost underrepresented perspectives rather than promoting only viral content. An AI could be created to detect harassment towards a user and limit visibility of these replies. There should be a hate speech filter, which allows users to filter these comments without silencing debates. Users should be held accountable for comments which they leave on peoples posts and platforms should be held accountable for allowing hate speech and bullying to occur within their platform.

Social media has created users to be obsessed with metrics (likes, follows, view counts), creating a toxic environment where users increasingly equate these numbers to their personal worth. By removing these metrics while still slowing users to follow accounts and see content, platforms could break this harmful cycle. Without these numbers, users might more authentically, focusing on meaningful interactions rather than performance metrics. This approach would remove the psychological pressure to constantly measure up to benchmarks.

An ethical internet must prioritize the safety of its most vulnerable users which are children. While platforms impose age restrictions, these measures are often ineffective, as young users easily bypass them, exposing themselves to predators and harmful content. Platforms must prioritize safety over engagement, ensuring protection for minors. Orgad mentions the internet functioning as a “mediapolis,” which is an ethical internet that has a duty of care to all users, especially the most vulnerable. (Orgad, 2007, pg. 33).

In reimagining an ethical internet, we need to move beyond current platforms that reward exclusion and superficial interactions. We need to create a digital space that is rooted in empathy, responsibility, and inclusivity. To create an ethical internet, means to intentionally design and rethink algorithms, revaluate platform priorities, and actively protect and amplify marginalised voices.

References

Orgad, S. (2007). The internet as a moral space: the legacy of Roger Silverstone. New Media & Society, 9(1), pp.33–41. doi:https://doi.org/10.1177/1461444807075202.
