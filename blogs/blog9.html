Reimagining the Internet: Without Leaving Anyone Behind

Introduction

This blog will be a critical reflection on Sheena Magenya’s reading, “Making a Feminist in Africa: Why the Internet Needs African Feminists and Feminism.” The article challenges the myth of the internet being a neutral and safe space and exposes how it mirrors the same inequalities that people fight offline, such as exclusion violence and systematic bias, particularly from African feminists.

The Internet isn't Neutral

Magenya points out that just because activism moves online these days doesn’t mean everyone gets an equal voice. During the COVID-19 pandemic, many African women, queer people, and disabled communities were left out in online meetings such as Zoom and faced online abuse. Having access to the internet is not enough, being heard on the internet is what is important. Our society is developing from old ways of thinking, but some of these mindsets are still instilled into our societies. It can be seen when the internet favours ‘powerful voices,’ usually white, male, and straight. By employing African feminists, they can make a change to this, where they aren’t just sharing content, but they are shifting power. Giving space to African women, feminists or queer individuals gives a voice to marginalized communities, allowing for people to share their stories and find communities.

Africa as a Tech Lab vs a Tech Creator

One of Magneya’s strongest arguments in this reading is how Africa is often seen as a place that receives technology and doesn’t create it. When Western society is developing tech for African countries, their focus is on solving problems like hunger or disease, but they ignore innovation for fun or creativity. African technology should be used as a tool to educate individuals about the vast cultures and introduce a creative and fun element to it. This matches what Chimamanda Adichie calls the “danger of a single story.” (Magenya, 2020, pg.5) Magenya states in the reading that “we do not innovate for fun.” Whenever a story about Africa pops up on my social media, it is full of sad stories, but where are the posts celebrating feminist creators or our rich culture? The internet hides these stories unless you search for them.

A Feminist Internet

What stays with me is Magenya’s vision of a feminist internet as a place for “more African women, more sexuality and diversity, more glitter and ochre.” She wants to focus on the safety of these individuals and to allow joy and freedom. This allows for a space where for example a gay man can do a makeup tutorial or a disabled woman can talk about sex, without receiving hate.

Critiques

Magenya’s reading centres on African feminist voices in reshaping the internet, it overlooks some contradictions. The piece critiques digital inequality but doesn’t fully address how material barriers (such as unreliable electricity or expensive data) limit feminist resistance in practice.

Conclusion

This article made me think about my own online habits. How does my own privilege (like having access to the internet) keep me from seeing others' struggles? Magenya calls not only for big organizations but for all of us to demand an internet where “access” means more than just connections; it means power. A feminist internet isn’t a dream, it’s a necessity.
