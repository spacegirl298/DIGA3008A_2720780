Decolonizing Code: Accountability in the Age of Algorithms

Decolonizing Code

When writing code, we’re not just creating functional websites, we are embedding biases and power structures into the systems that shape society. After reading Mohamed and Berhane’s’ readings, it forced me to reflect on how my own work could perpetuate coloniality.

Mohamed highlights in his reading, “Decolonial AI: Decolonial Theory as Sociotechnical Foresight in Artificial Intelligence,” how AI systems often replicate colonial power structure by centring Westerns norms as universal. For example, how facial recognition tools are predominantly coded and tested on light-skinned users and causes it to fail for darker skin users. (Mohamed et. al, 2020, pg.9) This is a design problem rooted in racism and exclusivity. It prioritizes a certain group of people and marginalizes a huge demographic. To avoid this in my own work, I need to ask myself: Who is represented here? I believe there is value in using existing code and datasets as resources, but with critical awareness. While using available tools, I must actively work to avoid replicating the same exclusionary practices prevalent in Western tech.

In Birhane’s reading, “Algorithmic Colonization of Africa,” he dismantles the myth of neutral AI. He highlights South Africa’s Vumacam surveillance system as an example. This system labels Black manual labourers as “suspicious” by default (Birhanne, 2020, pg.18). Birhane states “how we see the world […] is reflected in the algorithmic models we build” (Birhane, 2020, pg.17). Algorithms don’t just process data, they encode the biases and worldviews of their creators. This forces me to confront my own blind spots as a creator. To avoid perpetuating colonial patterns in tech I need to document the biases and limitations of my code transparently.

Mohamed’s concept of “reverse tutelage” (Mohamed et.al, 2020, pg.16) fundamentally shifts how we approach the development of technology, rather than imposing solutions on communities, we must learn from their lived experiences. When developing software for any community, I commit to involving community members as equal collaborators in decision making, not just as end-users.

Moving forward in my career as a creator I need to recognise that decolonising code isn’t about guilt, it’s about accountability. As Mohamed urges, we need a “critical technical practice” (Mohamed et. al, 2020, pg. 14) that bridges activism and coding. For me, this means learning from postcolonial critiques and advocating for policies that protect against algorithmic exploitation.

Incorporating JavaScript

I have not implemented much JavaScript at the moment as I have been focusing on redesigning my website, making sure it reflects my personality and artistic style, which I feel like my original website lacked. I’m slowly implementing JavaScript to enhance the experience without overwhelming users. I ma not selectively adding subtle interactions to enhance usability.
